Automated Semantic Drift Detection and Correction in Large-Scale Text Datasets via Hyperdimensional Graph Embedding and Bayesian Calibration
**Abstract:** The deterioration of semantic coherence within large-scale text datasets over time, known as semantic drift, poses a significant challenge to model stability and generalizability. Our approach constructs a knowledge graph representing semantic relationships within the dataset, utilizes HDGE to capture latent semantic properties, and employs Bayesian calibration to dynamically adjust weighting factors for drift mitigation. **2. Theoretical Foundations**

**2.1. Hyperdimensional Graph Embedding (HDGE)**

HDGE represents entities (words, phrases, sentences) as high-dimensional vectors (hypervectors). Semantic similarity is then encoded by the cosine similarity between these hypervectors. We adapt this approach by constructing a dynamic knowledge graph where nodes represent tokens, and edges indicate semantic relationships derived from co-occurrence statistics and pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, GloVe, BERT embeddings). The graph is updated incrementally as new data points are added, allowing for the ongoing reflection of evolving semantic landscapes. The HDGE process can be formally represented as follows:

* **Hypervector Initialization:** Each token *t* is initialized with a random, orthogonal hypervector *ht* ∈ ℝD, where *D* is a high dimension (e.g., 10,000).

* **Graph Traversal & Hypervector Combination:** For each token, its hypervector is updated through a recursive traversal of its neighbors in the knowledge graph. Combining a sequence of hypervectors is done using the circular convolution operation:

ℎ = ℎ₁ ⊙ ℎ₂ ⊙ … ⊙ ℎₘ
h =h₁⊙h₂⊙…⊙h_m

Where ⊙ represents the circular convolution operator.

**2.2. Bayesian Calibration for Drift Mitigation**

Bayesian calibration provides a principled framework for dynamically adjusting weighting factors within a drift correction pipeline. We model the probability of a given token’s semantic representation accurately reflecting its true meaning as a beta distribution. This distribution is updated based on observed performance metrics (e.g., downstream task accuracy, semantic similarity scores to reference embeddings) within a sliding window. Tokens with low Bayesian probability are flagged for correction.

* **Beta Prior:** Each token *t* is initialized with a Beta prior distribution β(*αt*, *βt*) representing prior knowledge about its semantic stability. Typical initial values might be β(2, 2), reflecting moderate prior belief in stability.

* **Likelihood Function:** The likelihood function, *L(*αt*, *βt* | data), represents the probability of observing the data given the current of token *t*.

* **Posterior Update:** The posterior distribution is calculated using Bayes’ Theorem: β(*α’t*, *β’t*) = *L*(*αt*, *βt* | data) * β(*αt*, *βt*)

## Commentary on Automated Semantic Drift Detection and Correction (ASDC)

This research tackles a crucial, growing problem in the age of massive language models: *semantic drift*. As datasets used to train these models age, the meanings of words and relationships between concepts subtly shift. This isn’t a sudden change; it’s a slow, insidious alteration that degrades model performance and introduces instability. The ASDC framework proposed in the paper offers an automated solution, combining Hyperdimensional Graph Embedding (HDGE) and Bayesian calibration to detect and correct this drift. Let’s break down how it works and why it’s significant.

**1. Research Topic Explanation and Analysis**

The core idea is that traditional AI training datasets aren’t static. Language evolves, cultural references change, and even the way we use words shifts over time. This impacts the accuracy of models trained on older data. Existing methods are often manual (requiring human review – impractical for large datasets) or rely on simplistic statistical measures (insufficiently sensitive to nuanced semantic changes). The ASDC framework aims to automate and improve upon these limitations.

The technologies chosen, HDGE and Bayesian calibration, are particularly well-suited to the problem. **HDGE** is an innovative approach to representing words and their relationships. It transcends simple word embeddings like Word2Vec or GloVe by constructing a *knowledge graph* where words are nodes and relationships are edges. Crucially, these relationships are captured not just by co-occurrence (words appearing together a lot), but also by how words relate to each other conceptually, drawing in pre-trained embeddings (BERT, for example) to leverage existing semantic knowledge. This allows HDGE to capture latent, complex semantic relationships, which is far superior to traditional co-occurrence matrices. A key technical advantage is HDGE’s ability to incrementally update its knowledge graph as new data arrives, dynamically reflecting evolving language patterns. Limitations arise when the static influencing embeddings (Word2Vec, GloVe, BERT) themselves are outdated, potentially introducing their own biases or failing to capture extremely recent shifts in language – a constant refreshing of these would be required.

**Bayesian calibration** acts as a smart filter within the framework. Instead of blindly correcting everything, it assesses the *certainty* of each word’s semantic representation. Using a Beta distribution, it throws tokens suspected of drifting into a “correction candidate” pool based on observed performance dips on downstream tasks. This probabilistic approach prevents overcorrection and spurious changes based on short-term fluctuations.

**2. Mathematical Model and Algorithm Explanation**

The **HDGE process** starts by assigning each token (word, phrase) a random, high-dimensional vector (hypervector), represented as *ht* ∈ ℝD (where *D* is a large number, like 10,000). The graph traversal and combination is where the semantic relationships are captured. Imagine a word “apple.” It’s connected to “fruit,” “red,” “sweet,” and maybe “technology” (referring to Apple Inc.). As the algorithm traverses the graph, it combines the hypervectors of these related nodes using a “circular convolution” operation (denoted by ⊙). This operation isn’t simple addition; it’s a complex mathematical process that preserves and amplifies the semantic relationships between the words. The equation ℎ = ℎ₁ ⊙ ℎ₂ ⊙ … ⊙ ℎₘ concisely represents this combination of hypervectors. Essentially, the final hypervector *h* represents a distilled, composite meaning derived from the interconnected nodes.

**Bayesian Calibration** utilizes Bayes’ Theorem. It starts with a *prior* belief about a token’s stability (the Beta prior β(*αt*, *βt*)). Let’s say *αt* and *βt* are initially set to 2, reflecting a moderate initial belief in stability. As the model performs tasks and observes errors, a *likelihood function* tells us how likely that data is given the current representation. Bayes’ Theorem then updates the *posterior* distribution (β(*α’t*, *β’t*)), increasing *α’t* if the token is performing well or decreasing *β’t* if it’s performing poorly – effectively adjusting the weighting of that token.
