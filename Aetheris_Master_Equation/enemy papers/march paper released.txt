This paper discusses different Hyper-Dimensional Computing (HDC) architectures and their utilization in solving
various artificial intelligence (AI) applications. HDC is based on the principles of high-dimensional vector spaces
and presents a distinct way of representing and processing information. The paper first presents an outline of the
fundamental principles, mathematical underpinnings, and essential operations of HDC, thereby establishing the
necessary basis for subsequent discussions. Afterward, the paper explores prominent conventional and modern
HDC architectures, clarifying their operational principles and practical uses. In addition, the paper also discusses
the performance evaluation of different HDC architectures, in terms of accuracy, efficiency, and scalability, in
solving real-world tasks. The limitations of existing HDC architectures were also examined in this paper, and
their plausible remedies are proposed. Lastly, the paper provides an analysis of future prospects, encompassing research trends to fully utilize HDC as an alternative to deep learning to solve different classification and
recognition tasks. Offering a bird’s-eye perspective of HDC architectures and their changing place in AI, this
paper presents a comprehensive survey for academics, industry professionals, and interested parties working on
AI-based applications.
1. Introduction
Artificial intelligence (AI) has experienced substantial growth recently,
fueled by the persistent exploration of innovative computing models that
can mimic and improve cognitive abilities. Hyper-Dimensional Computing (HDC), also known as Vector Symbolic Architecture (VSA), is
a computational framework that draws inspiration from the characteristics of high-dimensional (HD) spaces. Unlike conventional computing
techniques that rely on discrete, low-dimensional representations, HDC
operates within a HD, continuous domain. In this domain, information is
encoded in vectors with extremely high dimensionality, typically spanning hundreds to thousands of dimensions. This approach leverages the
mathematical properties of HD space to efficiently and reliably perform
complex computations [1–4].
The drive to investigate HDC arises from the need to surpass the constraints of traditional AI architectures, specifically in managing sophisticated and complex data representations inherent in real-world applications. Conventional models often struggle with handling noise, scaling

1.2. Motivation of using hyper-dimensional computing for AI applications
There are multiple factors that motivate the investigation of HDC in AI
applications:
1. The methods for processing and representing large data using HD
vectors are well organized and extensive. In contrast to traditional
techniques, the challenge of handling the dimensionality of complex
data is efficiently managed by HDC by using the attributes of HD
spaces while ensuring computational efficiency. Adapt HDCs for applications consisting of large data and require real-time processing
[8].
2. Because of high-dimensionality, a huge advantage of HDC is its robustness to noise and errors. This HD representation can bear a notable amount of noise, while maintaining the integrity of encoded
information. For cases of real-world environments where data is incomplete or altered by noise, these characteristics of HDC become
more pivotal and decisive [9].
3. Human cognition, where information is saved and processed in HD
space, is the inspiration principle behind HDC. As AI systems are also
based on human-like reasoning and learning, HDC is a suitable and
potential candidate to develop these AI systems. If cognitive methods and computational methods are synchronized, HDC is capable of
more insightful and explicable AI systems [1,7,10].
4. The feature of adaptability and resourcefulness makes HDC applications diversified in many fields. Comprehensive and contextconscious AI systems are possible as a result of this property to encode different types of data into HD space. This multidisciplinary
usefulness highlights the applicability of HDC to diverse fields [11].
1.3. Current trends and research focus
The effectiveness and accuracy of HD representations is pivotal in HDC.
Research is being conducted for producing efficient encoding schemes.
An example of this can be hybrid encoding schemes, where binary,
ternary and continuous values are integrated to better capture different variations of sophisticated data structures. These studies are focused
on improving HDC HD representational abilities while maintaining the
computational performance.
Researchers are exploring the concept of fusion of neural networks with
HDC in order to achieve the advantages of both frameworks. The main
advantage of HDC over neural networks lies in its ability to generate effective symbolic representations. On the other hand, neural networks excel at learning from massive datasets using gradient-based optimization.
The complex AI applications that require both of these strengths can be
developed by hybrid modeling of HDC and neural networks [7,10]. Another active research area in HDC is to design models as an alternative to
deep learning and natural language processing systems forspecific applications. For instance, researchers are developing HDC methods that can
encode and exploit linguistic structures. This is crucial for natural language processing tasks like machine translation, sentiment analysis, and
text classification [12]. On the other hand, for genetic diseases classification in bio-informatics, HDC models are being developed to examine
gene-related data and to detect patterns [13,14]
Apart from this, HDC is also used in conjunction with different hardware
acceleration methods. This is essential to meet the computational requirements of HDC. Specialized hardware, such as Field-Programmable
Gate Arrays (FPGAs) and neuromorphic chips, is used to implement HDC
algorithms. The primary objective of utilizing these hardware solutions
is to enhance HDC performance by enabling real-time processing and reducing energy consumption. These two parameters are critical in edge
devices and embedded systems [8,15,16].
HDC is revolutionary in the field of AI. For information processing and
representation, and by using the properties of HD spaces, HDC presents
a robust, flexible and expandable solution. The current research in
the field of encoding schemes, neural network fusion, application-wise
model adjustment, and hardware acceleration techniques is the driving
force behind designing innovative AI applications.

2. Hyper-dimensional computing basics
2.1. Overview of hyper-dimensional computing
HDC is a novel approach specifically for AI applications that differs
from traditional methods by utilizing the inherent characteristics of HD
spaces. HDC primarily uses HD vectors and the information is encoded
in binary or ternary formats. Data representation, data transformation,
and data recovery are crucial components of any computing system.
Classical computing specifically involves the manipulation of bits. Each
bit has a binary value of either 0 or 1. This can be made possible through
the absence or presence of electric charge. Data transformation is an essential aspect of computation. The ALU performs logical operations and
the four basic arithmetic operations: addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division to compute new data [2]. This data is read/written
by main memory. HD computing utilizes hyper-vectors as its data type,
which typically have a high dimensionality in the thousands, in contrast to classical computing. These broad words are resistant to noise
interference, making them inherently strong and reliable. The primary
source of inspiration for HDC is derived from Pentti Kanerva’s research
in [1], representing entities using HD vectors and manipulating them
using conventional computing methods. This section offers a detailed
introduction to the fundamental principles and essential features that
define HDC.
2.2. HDC notions
2.2.1. Hyper-vectors
In HDC, the hyper-vector serves as the basic building block with three
significant properties. First, hyper-vectors or HD numerical vectors, typically consist of more than 10,000 dimensions, with each dimension
representing a numerical value. This offers a vast expanse for hypervectors to represent information [20]. Secondly, hyper-vectors are typically initialized in a random manner, and the generated numbers adhere to independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) randomness. It
is likely that two randomly initialized hyper-vectors will be nearly orthogonal to each other due to this high dimensionality. Third, hypervectors possess a holographic nature in which all dimensions within an
hyper-vector are acknowledged and valued equally, without any dimension being deemed more significant than others. Put simply, the hypervector should be regarded as a cohesive entity rather than being broken
down into micro-code. An hyper-vector with 𝐷 dimension is shown as:

2.3. HDC steps for model development:
The development of an HDC model usually involves four main stages
[20], as shown below.
– Encoding: Encoding is the fundamental stage in the development of
the HDC model. During the encoding process, the input samples are
transformed into their corresponding hyper-vectors using a specific
set of application-dependent HD operations and the item memories.
Detailed information on the main HDC encoding schemes is discussed in Section 2.4. Following the encoding process, the realistic
features are transformed into HD representations known as hypervectors. These hyper-vectors are subsequently utilized in the remaining phases of the development of the HDC model.
– Training: During the training phase, associative memory is trained
using encoding hyper-vectors derived from training samples. The AM
combines all sample hyper-vectors with the same label 𝑐 and adds
them to the respective class hyper-vector 𝑋∗
𝑐
. The purpose of this
task is to gather and combine information in order to construct a
representative hyper-vector for each class.
– Inference: Inference is the stage in which AM is used to guess the
class of unrevealed test samples. During the process of inference, the
unrevealed samples are also encoded using item memory. These encoded hyper-vectors are normally called query hyper-vectors. Using
a similarity metric, the similarity between each class hyper-vector
in the AM and the query hyper-vector is computed. For this unseen
sample, the class with the highest resemblance is determined as the
predicted class.
– Retraining: Retraining is an elective step in refining the already
trained AM to obtain greater performance of the model. During the
retraining process, the HDC model iterates over the already encoded
or trained samples and uses the trained AM to obtain prediction
labels in inference. Next, the predicted label is compared to the
ground-truth label to determine if there is a difference. In the event
of any discrepancy, the AM is updated.
2.4. Encoding schemes
Binary encoding is a crucial component of HDC, in which binary digits are used for information representation, specifically 0s and 1s. This
encoding system is straight-forward and effective, which has led to its
widespread adoption in several HDC architectures. These binary representations support bit-wise OR and AND, which are efficient vector
operations for better information handling.
Ternary encoding, unlike binary encoding, incorporates an extra state,
usually represented as -1, in addition to states 0 and 1. The use of ternary
representations enables more complex encoding and improves the expressiveness of hyper-dimensional vectors. Ternary encoding is especially beneficial in applications that require a more precise and detailed
representation. The most commonly used data mapping techniques for
hyper-dimensional space [31] are listed below.
– Binary: Here HD elements contains values of 0,1.
– Ternary: Here HD elements contains values of 0,1,-1.
– Non-binary HD vector: The elements in these vectors are normally
indicated by using a floating-point, fixed-point, or complex number.
– Dense HD vector: The probability distribution of each HD element
is uniform in the dense HD vector.
– Sparse HD vector: With few instances of 1 or -1, the HD vector is
dominated by the value 0 in the sparse model.
Siamese [32] is a unique non-linear encoding scheme that uses Siamese
Neural Network (SNN) [33] to transfer scalar characteristics into binary
hyper-vectors. This mapping is based on user-specified cosine similarity drop-offs. The SNN receives a pair of scalar features as input and
produces a pair of bipolar hyper-vectors, having the values from the set
-1, +1. To generate these encoding representations, the SNN is trained
using a mean squared error (MSE) loss function, which measures the
similarity between the input and output pairs.

3. Major hyper-dimensional computing architectures
The evolution of HDC has been influenced by both traditional and modern architectures (see Fig. 2). This section offers a brief overview of
prominent HDC architectures, emphasizing their significant contributions.
3.1. Tensor product representations (TPR)
One of the pioneering models in HDC is the Tensor Product Representations, also known as the TPR model. Smolensky et al. [44] in 1990,
initially introduced the TPR model. Eduardo Mizraji [45,46] also proposed similar ideas during the same era; however, they did not attract
significant interest from the scientific community. Smolensky et al. [44]
employs the tensor product, which is the outer product of specified vectors, to generate a representation that integrates all the information from
the inputs. A normalized inner product (tensor product) is needed to be
computed of matrix and vector in order to extract the input information
from the generated matrix. This approach results in appropriate and accurate binding and unbinding. However, the utilization of the tensor
product leads to an issue: the result of combining two vectors through
the tensor product is a matrix, and the dimensions of this matrix increase
with each successive computation. When using HDC/VSA models, it is
expected that the dimensionality of the result hyper-vectors be identical
to the argument hyper-vectors in order to ensure engineering practicality and facilitate mathematical computations. Therefore, the classification of the TPR model as an HDC model depends on whether the fixed
dimensionality of representations is regarded as a mandatory characteristic of an HDC model or not. TPR can be viewed as HDC forerunner.

3.4. Matrix binding of additive terms (MBAT)
The Matrix Binding of Additive Terms (MBAT) model, presented by Gallant et al. [5], suggests using matrix vector multiplication to carry out
the binding action. This option was briefly covered in the Plate book
[55] as well. Typically, it has the ability to alter the dimensionality of
the resulting hyper-vector , if necessary. Atomic hyper-vectors have a

hyper-vector must be verified. The matrix should be multiplied by the
hyper-vector , and the similarity of the resulting hyper-vector should be
calculated and compared to a predefined similarity threshold.

3.5. Task-projected hyper-dimensional computing (TP-HDC)
Chang et al. [56] introduced Task-Projected Hyper-Dimensional Computing (TP-HDC) as a method to enable the HD model to handle several
tasks at the same time by utilizing excess dimensionality in the hyperspace. The TP-HDC framework is shown in Fig. 6. In order to update
distinct Associative Memories (AMs) for every task in a task sequence,
the methodology uses TP-HDC. In the inference stage, the HD queries are
generated. TP-HDC achieves a 12.8% increase in accuracy compared to
the baseline method and successfully reduces information loss between
tasks by using redundant dimensionality in the hyperspace.
3.6. VoiceHD
Imani et al. [27] introduced an innovative design called VoiceHD, aimed
at achieving energy efficiency and fast speech recognition to classify
26 letters from the spoken dataset. VoiceHD uses a similarity check to
categorize voices after encoding them into HD space. The VoiceHD system consists of two primary components: an encoder and an associative
memory. VoiceHD architecture is shown in Fig. 7.
Initially, the voice signal is converted into the frequency domain, where
it is represented by 𝑁 frequency ID channels and 𝑀 levels. Subsequently, VoiceHD translates the ID and level information into arbitrary
hyper-vectors that are stored in the item memory. During the training
process, the VoiceHD encoding module combines these hyper-vectors to
form learned patterns. These patterns correspond to 26 hyper-vectors,
which are then saved in the AM. During the testing process, query hypervectors are produced using a similar encoding module. These query
hyper-vectors are then transmitted to AM. The query hyper-vector is
compared to the stored hyper-vectors of 26 classes. The hyper-vector
with the highest similarity is selected to forecast the letter. The dimensionality 𝐷, of the hyper-vectors is 10,000.
3.7. QuantHD
Imani et al. [57] introduced a novel approach for quantizing the HD
computing model during training called QuantHD. While maintaining
the classification accuracy, QuantHD converts the trained non-binary
hyper-vectors to binary (0, 1) or ternary (−1,0,+1) models to represent
the class hyper-vectors. As shown in Fig. 8, QuantHD comprises three
primary stages: 1) Initial Training: This process involves creating an initial HD model by aggregating all encoded hyper-vectors that correspond
to each class. The initial training step follows the same process as the
conventional HD computing methodologies. 2) Quantization: is a process that converts the HD model to a binary or ternary model. Due to
the HD model being trained to process floating-point numbers, quantization leads to a substantial degradation in quality. Furthermore, 3)
Retraining: is used, with a pre-defined iteration number of 30, to counteract the accuracy degradation caused by quantization. The similarity
check now is either the dot product (ternary model) or the Hamming distance (binary model) rather than the cosine metric (non-binary model).
Fig. 9 represents the Binary/Ternary models.
3.8. NeuralHD
Zou et al. [58] presented NeuralHD, an innovative HDC method featuring a dynamic encoder for adaptable learning. The process finds and
eliminates irrelevant dimensions (least impact on learning) from the
computation, hence improving accuracy by reconstructing them on the
encoding module. Fig. 10 displays a NeuralHD learning framework. NeuralHD initially transforms the data into an HD space using one of the
available encoding techniques. NeuralHD trains the encoded data and
normalizes the trained model to simplify the similarity measure used in
the inference/retraining process, specifically the dot product operation.
Next, NeuralHD calculates the variance between different class dimensions to identify the dimensions with minimal effect on the accuracy of
classification. The NeuralHD algorithm eliminates irrelevant dimensions
from both the model and the base hyper-vectors in the encoder module.
NeuralHD ultimately regenerates the fundamental hyper-vectors in the
specified dimensions.

3.10. SearcHD
Imani et al. [59] introduced a comprehensive binary HD computing algorithm called SearcHD, which is capable of performing all HD computing tasks, including training, encoding, and inference, exclusively
using binary operations. Various encoding methods exist for mapping
data points into an HD space, such as n-gram-based and record-based
encoding. In this context, SearcHD employs record-based encoding, a
method that is particularly advantageous for hardware implementation
and relies solely on bitwise operations. This encoding method identifies
the lowest and highest values of the features and then divides that range
into 𝑚 equal-spaced levels. Next, it assigns a randomly generated binary
hyper-vector of dimensions 𝐷 to each of the quantized levels.
In the field of HD computing, the likelihood that two randomly generated data hyper-vectors are orthogonal to each other is high. During the
training process, hyper-vectors representing data belonging to the same
class are modified to be similar to each other. SearcHD is a framework
(shown in Fig. 12) that facilitates the binarization of the HD computing
technique in the training and inference stages. SearcHD eliminates the
addition operation during training by utilizing bitwise substitution. This
method trains a model by randomly sharing the query hyper-vectors’
elements with each class hyper-vector. The binary model with HD computing has a limited level of accuracy in classification, so SearcHD takes
advantage of vector quantization to describe an HD model employing
many vectors for each class. SearcHD is able to increase the amount
of information stored in each class without changing the model from
binary vectors.
3.11. GrapHD
Poduval et al. [60] introduced a hyper-dimensional graph memory
called GrapHD that makes holographic, effective, and reliable cognitive
learning possible. GrapHD encodes different types of graph data in a HD
space. The encoding is based on a clearly defined set of mathematical
principles. GraphHD encoding utilizes a solitary hyper-vector to represent a graph, with each dimension representing a neuron. Numerous
cognitive operations are possible directly over the graph hyper-vector
with GrapHD. The exact information from the graph is extracted by these
cognitive operations without directly accessing the original nodes. The
authors utilize these functionalities to facilitate many applications, such
as graph matching, finding the shortest path, and detecting objects. The

3.12. Hardware and system-level architectures
Beyond algorithmic formulations, a growing body of work has focused
on realizing HDC efficiently at the hardware and system level, targeting
edge intelligence, energy efficiency, and scalability. Several studies have
explored algorithm-hardware co-design approaches, where HDC primitives are mapped onto existing accelerator infrastructures to exploit data
parallelism and reduce memory access overheads. Ni et al. [61] propose
an algorithm-hardware co-design framework that maps HDC operations
onto existing low-power accelerators, specifically the Google Edge TPU,
by interpreting HDC as a hyper-wide neural network. Gupta et al. [62]
introduce FELIX, a processing-in-memory (PIM) architecture that enables single-cycle Boolean logic operations directly within memristive
crossbar memory, drastically reducing data movement for HDC. By executing HDC encoding and associative operations fully in memory, FELIX
demonstrated the strong suitability of HDC for in-memory architectures.
Khalegi et al. [17] present tiny-HD, a dedicated and configurable ASIC
engine for HDC tailored to ultra-low-power IoT applications through
algorithm-hardware co-optimization and simplified dataflow.

4. Utilization of HDC in different AI applications
The flexibility and ability to adapt of HDC architectures have resulted
in their effective utilization in different areas of AI.
This section presents the challenges of using HDC models to handle various tasks, along with solutions. A progressive and chronological timeline
of HDC applications is shown in Fig. 1. Due to the extensive utilization
of HDC in AI applications, we can group them into three categories: supervised learning, unsupervised learning and semi-supervised learning.
A typical example of an HDC application for supervised learning is text
classification [74]. By encoding and categorizing large text corpora using HD representations, computational resources can be significantly
reduced while still achieving competitive accuracy. Another example
can be found in the domain of sentiment analysis, where HDC models
possess the ability to comprehend intricate linguistic patterns and contextual subtleties in order to categorize sentiment polarity [75]. These
models gain advantages from the resilience of HDC against noise and its
efficiency in handling input spaces with high dimensions. Other applications in supervised learning include [19,76–78].

sensor data analysis, HDC-based models have the ability to recognize
patterns and identify anomalies without requiring labeled data. They
achieve this by utilizing the HD space to distinguish subtle variations
and irregularities. This feature is especially valuable in fields such as
industrial monitoring and network security, as it can identify abnormal
patterns and thereby prevent potential risks and malfunctions. Some
applications in unsupervised learning are [79,80].

HDC is the appropriate solution due to its robustness to noise and ability to manage different data types.
While classification remains a dominant use case in the literature, the
HDC has been successfully extended to other problem domains like clustering and recommendation systems. Imani et al. [81] propose HDCluster, an unsupervised clustering framework that extends HDC by iteratively forming and refining cluster hyper-vectors using similarity-based
associative updates. By leveraging the robustness and noise tolerance
of HD representations, HDCluster achieves competitive clustering accuracy with significantly lower computational and memory overhead
compared to conventional clustering methods. Guo et al. [82] introduce
HyperRec, an HDC-based recommender system that encodes users and
items into hyper-vectors and performs recommendation through similarity search in HD space. The approach enables efficient, scalable, and
privacy-friendly recommendations with low memory footprint and fast
inference, making it well-suited for edge and resource-constrained deployment scenarios.

6. Limitations of existing HDC architectures
Numerous interesting avenues and research directions arise as HDC in
AI continues to develop and become more popular. This section examines the limitations of current HDC architectures, identifying specific
areas in which researchers can investigate to improve the capabilities
and usefulness of this paradigm.
Despite the fact that it has promising capabilities, HDC is faced with a
number of significant limitations that have not yet been resolved. Scalability is a major obstacle when dealing with extremely large datasets
or highly complex tasks, as the size and complexity of the data can result in excessive computational and memory demands. Noise handling
is a significant concern when it comes to HDC models, as they may face
difficulties in maintaining accuracy and resilience in the presence of
excessive noise or when handling input data that is highly variable.
Moreover, the issue of interpretability persists; although HDC can effectively analyze and process HD data, the resultant models frequently
lack transparency, thereby complicating the comprehension and explanation of their decision-making mechanisms. This presents difficulties
in areas that require a high degree of explainability and accountability,
such as autonomous and healthcare systems. Furthermore, the continuous development of dynamic and adaptive encoding schemes that can
customize representations for particular tasks or data types is still an active research area. More comprehensive comparative studies and benchmarking against a wider range of cutting-edge models are also required
to create standardized evaluation procedures and identify precise situations in which HDC exceeds or underachieves current methods. The integration of HDC with current AI frameworks and infrastructure presents
practical difficulties, as HDC’s distinct computational paradigm may not
easily align with traditional architectures and toolsets employed in machine learning and AI. To ensure wider acceptance and progress of HDC
in various AI applications, it is essential to address these limitations.
Although the field of research on merging HDC with vision-language
models is developing, much work has not been done specifically to combine these two technologies. However, researchers have investigated the
potential and have taken initial measures to combine vision-language
models with HDC. Recent studies have shown that HDC can be efficiently utilized for multi-modal sensor fusion and integration of sensory
data into a cohesive representation. For example, studies in robotics
have shown that HDC has the ability to effectively combine perception
and motor commands, making it suitable for active perception tasks that
require integration of vision and language. Moreover, the utilization of
hyper-dimensional vectors for the purpose of representing and binding
multi-modal data, such as images and related descriptive text, implies
that HDC can function as a link between linguistic and visual information [9,134,207,208].
Although specific applications of vision-language models using HDC are
still being developed, these initial studies demonstrate the potential of
HDC to improve the representation and processing of visual and linguistic data when combined. This has the potential to drive progress in areas
such as scene understanding, where a model using natural language can
comprehend and articulate visual scenes, or interactive AI systems that
can comprehensively understand and respond to both visual and verbal
input. Additional studies and experiments in this field are expected to
produce more definitive models and applications, broadening the scope
and potential of AI systems utilizing HDC. The challenges and limitations associated with HDC that are discussed in this section can be resolved by implementing some of the strategies discussed in the future
work section (Section 7).
7. Future work
7.1. Research trends:
Further investigation could examine the incorporation of HDC into other
AI paradigms, resulting in hybrid models that capitalize on the advantages of multiple approaches. Hybrid models have the potential to improve performance and versatility through the integration of deep learning, symbolic reasoning, reinforcement learning, and HDC. Exploring
dynamic encoding schemes that are adaptable to the unique attributes
of particular tasks has the potential to improve the versatility of HDC
models. Research in this area could investigate methods for automatically updating encoding parameters according to the characteristics of
input data, enabling more flexible and context-aware representations.
Future research could prioritize the investigation of HDC in interdisciplinary applications, expanding its scope beyond the conventional domains of AI . Applications in disciplines such as social sciences, biology,
and neuroscience have the potential to reveal new knowledge and aid
in the creation of HDC models designed to address specific interdisciplinary problems.
7.2. Potential applications
Although HDC shows potential in many areas, its full potential is still
mostly untapped in a number of applications. HDC can be used in the
field of financial fraud detection to examine large datasets with high

7.3. Hardware acceleration
With the increasing prevalence of HDC models, specialized hardware
architectures specifically designed to meet their computational needs
can be developed. Research in this area could investigate the development of customized hardware accelerators designed to efficiently carry
out HDC tasks, thereby improving both speed and energy efficiency. Future research may explore the potential integration of HDC with quantum computing paradigms, considering the quantum-inspired nature of
certain HDC principles. Investigating the potential of utilizing quantum
computing resources to improve the capabilities of HDC models offers
an interesting opportunity for further research.
7.4. Interdisciplinary approaches
The collaboration between neuroscientists and AI researchers has the
potential to enhance the understanding of HDC and its possible links
to biological neural processes. These interdisciplinary approaches have
the potential to generate valuable insights that can benefit both fields
and advance the creation of brain-inspired AI models. It is crucial to
address the ethical and social implications as HDC applications become
more and more popular. Future research could investigate the societal
consequences of HDC models, including concerns related to bias, fairness, and transparency. It could also focus on creating guidelines for the
responsible deployment of these models.
In conclusion, the future of HDC presents a plethora of exciting
prospects, encompassing improved model capabilities, innovative applications, and interdisciplinary collaborations. Researchers are urged

HDC and make significant contributions to the progress of AI .
8. Conclusion
This paper provides a thorough examination of HDC frameworks and
their applications in AI. HDC has become a promising paradigm that
offers distinct advantages in how information is represented and processed, from its fundamental principles to practical applications. We
initiated our journey by conducting a thorough analysis of the fundamental principles of HDC. We explored the theoretical foundations, comprehending the complexities of encoding schemes, mathematical principles, and fundamental operations such as binding and superposition.
The analysis of prominent HDC architectures offered a thorough understanding of the development of this domain. Progressing further, we
explored a wide range of applications in different fields of AI . HDC
demonstrated its versatility and effectiveness in various fields such as
natural language processing, computer vision, speech processing, and
beyond. The paper covers the fundamental aspects of HDC, including
the main encoding schemes, as well as the results and dataset benchmarking. The limitations of HDC are also addressed in the paper. The
impact of HDC on the wider field of AI is significant. The paradigm’s
capacity to comprehend intricate connections in HD spaces has the potential to fundamentally transform our approach to tasks spanning from
data processing to decision-making. Given its proven success in various
applications, as well as the current research trends and future prospects,
HDC is poised to play a crucial role in the ongoing advancement of AI .
The incorporation of HDC into interdisciplinary domains, such as quantum computing and neuroscience, offers promising prospects for the exchange and blending of concepts and approaches. The possibility of creating XAI methods and customized hardware architectures designed for
HDC increases its chances of being widely accepted. The potential for
integrating HDC with vision-language models is very promising. This
integration has the potential to advance multi-modal AI systems by improving the efficiency and robustness of data representation and enhancing the scaling capabilities of large-scale language-vision tasks. By doing
so, the ambiguity in complex datasets and noise can be handled more
effectively. The integration of HDC’s HD encoding with vision-language
models has the potential to drive significant advancements in various applications, including context-aware robotics, visual question answering
and image captioning. This combination can result in more robust and
adaptable AI solutions by leveraging HDC’s encoding capabilities and
the contextual understanding of vision-language models. As we wrap
up this survey, it is crucial to recognize the challenges and limitations
that come with the progress in HDC. The researchers can ensure the reliability and robustness of HDC models by addressing various aspects such
as noise handling, memory requirements, scalability and interpretability. The combined efforts of researchers, interdisciplinary experts and
practitioners will be crucial in fully harnessing the capabilities of HDC.
As this domain develops further, we expect a diverse range of research
contributions, innovations, and practical uses that will strengthen HDC
as a powerful influence in the field of AI . HDC is a remarkable example of researchers pushing the limits of computational paradigms in the
constantly changing field of AI. The journey does not conclude at this
point but rather expands into unexplored areas, where the combination
of theoretical comprehension and practical implementations will persist
in shaping the future of HDC and its contribution to the advancement
of AI .
