Abstract

The rigorous deployment of autonomous agents is frequently bottlenecked by the compu-
tational intractability of verifying safety constraints in high-dimensional state spaces. While

recent advancements in Vector Symbolic Architectures (VSA) have successfully addressed
the challenge of semantic drift in Large Language Models, a parallel and equally critical
fragility remains in the kinematic control of autonomous decision-making. As agents scale
from simple chatbots to persistent control systems, the “Curse of Dimensionality” renders

traditional deterministic verification methods, such as grid-based Hamilton-Jacobi reach-
ability, computationally prohibitive, with complexity scaling exponentially, O((1/η)

n), for

degrees of freedom n > 5.
This paper introduces Constrained High-Dimensional Barrier Optimization (CHDBO),
a unifying framework that generalizes strict topological safety into the probabilistic domain.

We propose three major advancements: (1) The generalization of strict topological defini-
tions into a probabilistic framework utilizing Monte Carlo Barrier Certificates (MCBC) to

verify the boundary consistency of safe manifolds where n ≥ 128; (2) A scalable “Orthogonal
Projection” mechanism that mitigates the exponential complexity of grid checks, reducing
verification sample complexity to O(1) relative to dimension (with linear computational cost
O(n) due to vector operations), by leveraging bounded Lipschitz continuity to guarantee the
detection of non-singular failure modes; and (3) The integration of a gradient-based utility
maximizer that ensures asymptotic convergence to global optima without violating the safety
envelope. By imposing a strict hierarchical priority of the safety constraint over the utility
objective, we demonstrate that aggressive performance and high-confidence safety are not
mutually exclusive. Simulation results confirm that CHDBO maintains high effective safety
rates in dynamic environments while preserving the utility required for high-performance
autonomy.
1 Introduction
The trajectory of artificial intelligence has shifted rapidly from reactive, token-prediction models

to persistent, autonomous Agentic Control Systems (ACS). This evolution has revealed a funda-
mental fragility in current architectural paradigms: the phenomenon of “Agent Drift” [1, 3, 39],

which can expose the system to adversarial exploits and alignment failures [10]. As systems
operate over extended temporal horizons, they suffer from a form of structural entropy where
behavioral stability and decision quality degrade.

In our previous work, Mitigating Large Language Model Context Drift via Holographic In-
variant Storage [1], we addressed the semantic component of this failure. We demonstrated that

by anchoring an agent’s identity in a Vector Symbolic Architecture (VSA) substrate [2], essen-
tially a holographic “Read-Only” memory, we could mathematically immunize the agent against

the corruption of its goals and personality [1]. While Vector Symbolic Architectures inherently
possess the high-dimensional noise tolerance required to solve this problem [33, 34], a distinct
but parallel challenge exists in the kinematic domain: dynamical control drift.

1

1.1 The High-Dimensional Control Divergence
Modern autonomous agents do not merely process text; they operate in high-dimensional state
spaces, mapping inputs to actions in continuous environments [4]. Whether controlling a swarm
of drones (n > 100) [8] or navigating the semantic embedding space of a multi-modal reasoning
engine (n = 12, 288 [27]) or visual transformer [40], the agent must constantly optimize for utility
while adhering to strict safety constraints (e.g., “Do not delete root files,” “Do not collide”).

The central objective of modern control theory is the synthesis of an agent that is simultane-
ously aggressive in its pursuit of goals and conservative in its avoidance of failure. Historically,

this has forced a binary choice:

1. Formal Methods (The Conservative approach): Techniques such as Control Bar-
rier Functions (CBFs) [4] and Hamilton-Jacobi reachability [18] offer absolute, provable

safety guarantees. However, these methods suffer from the “Curse of Dimensionality” [20].
Verifying a safety manifold via grid discretization scales as O((1/η)
n
), where η is the grid
resolution and n is the dimension. For a simple robotic arm (n = 3), this is tractable; for
a semantic reasoning agent (n = 128), it is physically impossible, requiring computation
exceeding the age of the universe.
2. Learning-Based Control (The Aggressive approach): Deep Reinforcement Learning
(DRL) offers high performance and scalability but often lacks axiomatic safety [19]. While
recent advancements in “Safe RL” and Shielding attempt to impose constraints [8, 13],
standard implementations often rely on “soft” reward penalties that do not strictly prevent
catastrophic failure states, leading to rare events where the agent maximizes utility by
gambling with safety.
1.2 The “Golden Manifold” and Probabilistic Relaxation
To resolve this dichotomy, we must extend the concept of the “Golden Manifold”, formally defined
in control theory as the Forward Invariant Safe Set S [4, 28], from the semantic domain into
the kinematic domain. In this geometric region, the agent remains invariant to microscopic
fluctuations, preserving its trajectory against external perturbations.
This paper proposes Constrained High-Dimensional Barrier Optimization (CHDBO). We
argue that for high-dimensional systems, absolute deterministic safety verification is a theoretical
luxury that prevents practical deployment. Instead, we introduce a probabilistic relaxation of
the safety condition. By trading the deterministic exactness of grid checks for probabilistic
certainty bounded by Lipschitz continuity, we can utilize Monte Carlo integration to estimate
the volume of the safe set.
By sampling the hypersphere of the agent’s immediate trajectory and applying statistical

bounds to the barrier function h(x), CHDBO certifies safety with confidence levels approach-
ing 1 − 10−6

. This effectively matches the reliability of deterministic methods while breaking
the exponential scaling law regarding sample complexity. While the total computational cost

scales linearly (O(n)) due to vector operations, the required sample count N becomes a con-
stant factor governed only by the desired confidence interval and the Lipschitz constant of the

barrier function. We explicitly categorize this verification as Probabilistic Safety (PAC-style),
distinguishable from the Absolute Safety of low-dimensional formal methods. While Lipschitz
continuity theoretically bounds the minimum volume of failure modes, we acknowledge that in
high-dimensional spaces (n = 128), the Concentration of Measure phenomenon implies that rare,
“spiky” failure regions (Black Swans) with probability mass below our sampling resolution (ε)
may remain undetected. Our method certifies that Pfail < ε with high confidence, but does not
guarantee the absence of singularities with occurrence rates lower than the sampling threshold
(e.g., 10−9
).

1.3 Asymptotic Utility and Orthogonal Projection
However, strict safety must not preclude mission completion; a theoretically safe agent that
remains immobile is operationally useless. Our second contribution is a geometric control law
that ensures Asymptotic Utility by functioning as a minimally invasive safety filter. We introduce
a steering mechanism that constrains the control input strictly within the tangent cone of the
safe set S.
When the nominal action derived from the utility function U(x) threatens to violate the
invariant set, CHDBO does not simply halt the system. Instead, it solves a high-dimensional
Quadratic Program (QP) to perform an Orthogonal Projection of the intent vector onto the
safety boundary. This enables the agent to traverse the "skin" of the safe manifold, maintaining
the maximum permissible velocity tangent to the constraint, thereby resolving the "Frozen
Robot" problem [30] characteristic of naive barrier implementations where conflicting constraints
induce stagnation.
1.4 Contributions
The specific contributions of this paper are:
• Generalization of Semantic Safety: We extend the invariant preservation principles
of [1] from holographic memory to continuous control manifolds.

• High-Dimensional Adaptation of CBF-QP: We adapt standard Control Barrier Func-
tion (CBF) formulations [4] to high-dimensional semantic spaces (n ≥ 128). We demon-
strate that the quadratic programming (QP) formulation remains computationally tractable

(O(n)) for real-time semantic steering, overcoming the perceived "Curse of Dimensional-
ity" in verification [20].

• Hierarchical Utility Integration: We provide a lexicographic control architecture that
strictly prioritizes safety constraints over utility maximization, ensuring that aggressive
performance and rigorous safety are hierarchically integrated rather than competing.

3 High-Dimensional Probabilistic Verification
The central contribution of this work is the assertion that absolute deterministic safety is a
luxury of low-dimensional systems, while high-confidence probabilistic safety is a necessity for
high-dimensional ones. In this section, we present the Monte Carlo Barrier Certificate (MCBC),
a method that circumvents the scaling limitations of grid-based verification by estimating the
volume of the safe invariant set S rather than delineating its exact boundary.
3.1 The Scaling Bottleneck
As established in Section 2, the computational cost of exact verification scales as O((1/η)
n
). To
contextualize this: verifying a standard 3-link robotic arm (n = 6) on a coarse grid requires 1012
evaluations. Verifying a swarm of 50 drones or a semantic embedding space (n ≥ 128) requires

10200 evaluations, rendering deterministic grid-based verification physically intractable regard-
less of available computing power. However, the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma [31] suggests

that the geometric structure of the safe set, specifically the distance to the nearest boundary,
can be preserved with high probability even in lower-dimensional projections or sparse random

samplings (see [9] for a simplified proof). This geometric concentration of measure is the theo-
retical foundation that allows our Monte Carlo approach to succeed where grid-based methods

fail.
To solve this, we shift our verification criterion from Worst-Case Invariance (is there any point
x where safety fails?) to PAC-Style Invariance (is the probability of encountering a failure state
bounded by ε?). We employ Hoeffding’s Inequality to bound the estimation error ε [21]. This
approach aligns with modern uncertainty quantification methods, such as Conformal Prediction
[11], and established randomized control frameworks [16], effectively constructing a probabilistic
envelope around the system dynamics rather than a rigid boundary.
3.2 Randomized Barrier Certification

(MCBC) engine algorithm. Building upon the foundational concept of Barrier Certificates in-

It is important to address the theoretical concern of Concentration of Measure in high-
dimensional spaces (n = 128), where safety violations could theoretically exist as “spiky” man-

this, we leverage the Lipschitz continuity of the system dynamics f(x)—estimated via spectral

strictly guaranteeing safety against such pathological geometries in sparse, non-convex semantic
spaces remains challenging with passive sampling. Consequently, while our framework provides

a robust probabilistic upper bound, future iterations of CHDBO should integrate Active Learn-
ing or Importance Sampling to explicitly bias the verification distribution towards the boundary

∂S, thereby amplifying the detection rate of rare, thin failure modes.

It may be argued that in high-dimensional spaces (n = 128), even Lipschitz-continuous fail-
ure regions could remain statistically undetectable due to the vastness of the sampling volume.

However, this assumes that the safety hazard is uniformly distributed across all dimensions. In
practical control systems, safety violations (e.g., collisions) are typically low-rank phenomena,

depending on a small subset of state variables (effective dimensionality def f ≪ n) while remain-
ing invariant to the others. Our projection mechanism leverages this structure, ensuring that

sampling efficiency scales with the complexity of the hazard, not the complexity of the agent.
Furthermore, we leverage the Lipschitz property of the system dynamics. We assume Lh is

boundary is safe, there exists a hyper-ball B(xi

6 Conclusion

The pursuit of autonomous systems that are both demonstrably safe and operationally aggres-
sive has long been a “zero-sum” game in control theory. To guarantee safety via Hamilton-Jacobi

reachability or grid-based verification, one historically had to sacrifice scalability, resigning for-
mal proofs to low-dimensional toy problems. Conversely, to achieve high utility via Reinforce-
ment Learning, one had to sacrifice guarantees, accepting a non-zero probability of catastrophic

failure, a phenomenon known as “mutational meltdown” or “agent drift”, in exchange for perfor-
mance.

This paper has introduced Constrained High-Dimensional Barrier Optimization (CHDBO),
a unified framework that fundamentally resolves this dichotomy. By shifting the verification
paradigm from deterministic enumeration (which scales exponentially, O((1/η)
n

)) to probabilis-
tic certification (which scales constantly, O(1)), we have broken the “Curse of Dimensionality”

that has plagued safety-critical autonomy for decades [20].
Our empirical results confirm three critical advancements:
1. Safety is Probabilistically Scalable: Through Monte Carlo barrier certificates bounded
by Lipschitz continuity, we can verify the boundary consistency of R

128 manifolds with high
statistical confidence (1 − δ), effectively mitigating the risk of rare failure modes.
2. Utility is Asymptotic: By projecting utility gradients onto the tangent cone of the safe
set, we ensure that agents do not merely survive, but thrive, converging to global optima
without violating invariant constraints.
3. The Trade-off is False: We have demonstrated that aggressive performance and rigorous
safety are not mutually exclusive. An agent can operate at the very edge of the “safe
envelope” without ever crossing the line into failure.

We conclude that the future of robust autonomy lies in the synthesis of semantic and kine-
matic stability. While Holographic Invariant Storage (HIS) provides the “invariant core” of the

agent, preserving its goals and personality against context drift, CHDBO provides the “geometric
constraint,” ensuring its actions remain within the bounds of safety. Together, these technologies
offer the blueprint for “Industrialized Persistence,” enabling the safe deployment of superhuman
autonomous agents capable of operating indefinitely without regression.
